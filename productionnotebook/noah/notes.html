Genisis

Alas, I don't remember the details, but My idea log says I made the entry for "Noah: The Culling" on November 5th, 2008. I was probably thinking, "Why doesn't this exist?"
Since then, I'd contemplated different scenes and narrative threads and had originally concieved of the idea in cinematic terms. In December of 2011, I put together the poster concept as the centerpiece for a series of posters shot in and around my birthday.

Dinosaurs

The dinosaur concept has been integral from the beginning. The idea is to basically take the basic idea of Young Earth creationism (YEC) and specifically the idea that man and dinos coexisted as a Jurassic Park like element without the need for bringing them back to life. We lived with the dinos... and it wasn't pretty. Where did they go? The flood wiped them out. Why? Because they were part of the problem.
I should mention that in most YEC I've seen, the dinos were like other animals and are generally said to have been on the Ark, but died after the deluge due to environmental change. The 'Young Earth' version here is straight up narritive awesomeness and not any theological change. I do hope however that Young Earthers could see this tale and say, "Yep, I can get behind that."

Unicorn

The unicorn was brought up by Christine Gibert shortly before the poster concept was set. There was a myth-meme regarding unicorns that the pair had been lost during the flood and the surviving unicorn had been granted eternal (bio) life as recognition for some sacrifice (and later tragically killed as part of the Authorian legends?).
I remember the story presented as something heard elsewhere, though I have not found a reference for the unicorn-Ark story, though it does mesh with the medieval myths surrounding unicorns. It doesn't really matter, though, it works and I like it. It works very well to have a recognized, noble, and now missing creature commit a great sacrifice in the story, and the unicorn fits the bill to a t.

Betrayal of Crows

In Volume V: The Ark, one key sub-plot I've toyed with is the betrayal of the crows. I believe I have some notes on some underlying mythology here. The idea that crows somehow failed, and while not damned were cursed (to wit, their association with witches and ill omens) fits well with existing myths.
Specifically, the crows as beasts associated with man are called upon to give warning should danger approach. However, the a crow falls asleep allowing an attack on the Ark. As a result, the crows are no longegr favored and there exists slight emnity forevermore.
The Western medieval/European and Christian myths mentioned here get to the idea.
